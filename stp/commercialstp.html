Commercial Sewage Treatment Plan

The technology of reed beds or constructed wetlands is a highly efficient and eco-friendly wastewater treatment system that offers a lower cost and carbon footprint compared to traditional methods. By utilizing waste to generate new resources, reed beds play a crucial role in the regeneration of the natural environment cycle. The primary concept involves the filtration and aeration of wastewater through various stages of sand filter basins that are populated with green wetland plants, resulting in the production of treated water. We integrate and apply this eco-friendly wetland principle across our diverse range of wastewater treatment plant designs.

Process Diagram

A Jal Science Bio-Reed Non-Electric Solution System for wastewater treatment typically uses natural processes involving plants and microbes to treat and purify water. This system mimics natural wetlands, utilizing the root zones of plants and microbial activity to break down contaminants. Here’s an overview of how it works:

1. Pre-Treatment

Wastewater is first pre-treated to remove large solids and debris. This can involve a primary settling tank where solids settle to the bottom and are removed.

2. Reed Bed System

Content to be given


3. Reed Beds

The core of the system consists of reed beds, which are shallow basins filled with gravel or soil and planted with specific reed species (like Phragmites australis).

4. Reed Bed/PGF

A shallow concrete tanks of graded gravel or pebbles and selected species of plants (such as Reed, Canna indica or Cyperus papyrus) to treat remaining pollutants by biological conversion, filtration and surface aeration. It can be integrated into the landscape.


5. Root Zone Activity

The roots of these plants provide a large surface area for microbial biofilms to develop. These microbes are crucial for breaking down organic matter in the wastewater.

6. Filtration

As wastewater flows through the reed bed, it is filtered by the substrate (gravel/soil), and contaminants are degraded by microbial action. The plants also absorb some of the nutrients from the wastewater.


7. Microbial Degradation

8. Aerobic and Anaerobic Zones

The system creates both aerobic (oxygen-rich) and anaerobic (oxygen-poor) zones. Different microbes thrive in these zones, contributing to the breakdown of a wide range of contaminants.


9. Biological Processes

Aerobic bacteria oxidize organic pollutants to carbon dioxide and water, while anaerobic bacteria reduce sulfates and nitrates, removing them from the water.

11. Plant Absorption

Plants in the reed bed absorb nutrients like nitrogen and phosphorus, which helps to reduce the nutrient load in the water and prevent eutrophication when the treated water is discharged into natural water bodies.

13. Final Settling

The water may pass through a final settling pond or tank to allow any remaining suspended solids to settle out.
